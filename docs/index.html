Antimicrobial Resistance Prediction in Salmonella enterica Using Frequency Chaos Game Representation and ResNet-18

Saifelden M. Ismail, Salma H. Fayed

Abstract

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) prediction from bacterial genomes remains a major challenge for clinical microbiology and surveillance. We developed deep learning models based on Frequency Chaos Game Representation (FCGR) and a ResNet-18 architecture to classify resistance phenotypes directly from whole-genome assemblies. Using homology-aware clustering to prevent genomic data leakage, we trained and evaluated models for Salmonella enterica (seven antibiotics) and Staphylococcus aureus (five antibiotics). The Salmonella enterica model achieved high predictive accuracy, particularly for cephalosporins, while performance was lower for tetracycline and ampicillin. The Staphylococcus aureus model demonstrated that the pipeline generalizes to Gram-positive bacteria, with strong results for methicillin (Balanced Accuracy = 0.85). Benchmarking against the gene-based tool ResFinder showed that the FCGR-based model did not match the performance of ResFinder on most antibiotics, but achieved competitive results for cephalosporins. This study demonstrates the feasibility of applying FCGR-based deep learning to AMR prediction across bacterial species, though substantial improvements would be needed before clinical application.

Materials and Methods

Comprehensive pipeline from genomic data to resistance predictions

1. Data Collection

Whole genome sequences were curated from NCBI and public repositories with validated phenotypic resistance profiles. Drugs selection was based on the labels Null% and Sensitive (S) to Resistant (R) ratio. Samples with no labels for selected drugs were excluded.

2. FCGR Encoding

Genomes were transformed into 256×256 grayscale images using 8-mer frequency analysis. This alignment-free encoding captures both local sequence motifs and global compositional biases.

3. Model Training

A modified ResNet-18 architecture was trained with homology-aware splitting and cross-validation. Two models were developed: (i) Salmonella model across 7 antibiotics, and (ii) a S. aureus model across 5 antibiotics.

4. Validation

Evaluation metrics included AUC, MCC, and balanced accuracy to account for incomplete resistance profiles. Confusion matrices and per-class metrics were generated for both models.

Results and Discussion

Performance evaluation across species and antibiotics

Salmonella Enterica Model

The Salmonella Enterica model achieved strong overall performance with balanced accuracy of 86% and AUC of 89%. Performance varied significantly across antibiotics: near-perfect predictions for cephalosporins (cefoxitin, ceftiofur, ceftriaxone) with balanced accuracy ≥94%, while tetracycline and ampicillin showed more modest performance (71-79% balanced accuracy).

Staphylococcus Aureus Model

The Staphylococcus Aureus model trained achieved balanced accuracy of 74% overall and AUC of 82%. Compared to Salmonella Enterica model, it achieves more modest performance, but nevertheless demonstrates that the FCGR-based pipeline is not restricted to a single species and can capture resistance-associated genomic patterns in a fundamentally different bacterial lineage.

Clinical and Biological Implications

Our deep learning approach demonstrates significant clinical potential for rapid AMR screening. Benchmarking against ResFinder revealed that FCGR-based models can approach curated gene-database predictions while potentially capturing distributed genomic features beyond known resistance genes. Saliency analysis showed that while the model recognizes established resistance genes like blaCMY-2, it relies on additional genomic motifs, suggesting potential for novel resistance mechanism discovery.

  • Reduction in diagnostic time from days to hours without requiring resistance gene annotations
  • Potential for discovering novel resistance mechanisms through distributed genomic patterns
  • Scalable deployment across diverse clinical and surveillance settings
